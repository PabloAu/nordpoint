Why NORD· exists

Markets are not magic — and you don’t need an expensive intermediary to participate

Stock markets are complex and highly competitive. No one can reliably beat them. What we do have is more than a century of data and a small set of robust ideas, documented in public research. This service exists to teach those ideas clearly, so you can build a long-term plan and understand results in real terms — after inflation, which is what determines your true purchasing power.

The core knowledge is public
The fundamentals of investing are well known and publicly available.You can learn them in a focused session — and apply them consistently for decades.
Long-term investing (10+ years)
Compounding becomes meaningful over long horizons. Over 10–30 years, modest real returns multiply your contributions and will have a significant impact in your finances.
Fees compound against you
Intermediaries do not have superior market knowledge. When you delegate, you pay recurring fees for only for convenience — and those fees compound against you over time.
Trading and tipsters
Active trading and stock picking consistently underperform the market, both before and after fees. The evidence is robust across markets and decades. In practice, trading is sustained by selling tips and content, not by long-term results.
Macroeconomics is worth learning
Finance and macroeconomics shape modern societies. Inflation, interest rates, growth, demographics, and productivity affect markets — and everyday life. Understanding them is part of understanding the world you live in.
Simple beats complex
Academic finance shows that effective investing is simple. There is broad consensus on sound practices. Complexity and narratives are often used to justify ongoing fees.

A focused, evidence-based approach

I am a senior scientist at ETH Zürich, working in biophysics. I approached investing for the same reason I approach science: to understand how complex systems behave using data, models, and the few ideas that reliably hold up over time. What surprised me was not how difficult long-term investing is, but how much of what matters is public knowledge — and how often it is obscured by unnecessary complexity and recurring fees.

  • Evidence first: markets, diversification, risk, and expected returns — grounded in decades of data rather than narratives or predictions.
  • Clarity through simplicity: a small set of well-established principles, explained clearly and applied consistently over long horizons.
  • Independence by design: no product sales and no percentage-based fees — just education, tools, and a framework you fully control.

Foundations

Pioneers, data, and public research

The principles used here are not proprietary. They are grounded in more than a century of public data, careful measurement, and peer-reviewed research. These ideas have been tested across markets, countries, and time periods. Despite major changes in technology and institutions, there is no empirical evidence that the fundamental principles governing markets have stopped applying or have been replaced. While future returns are never guaranteed, it is reasonable to expect that long-term real returns will continue to reflect economic productivity, growth, and risk.

Market foundations
  • Markowitz (1952) — Portfolio Selection.
  • Sharpe (1964) — Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM).
  • Samuelson (1965) — Proof That Properly Anticipated Prices Fluctuate Randomly.
  • Fama (1970) — Efficient Capital Markets.
  • Merton (1973) — Intertemporal Capital Asset Pricing Model.
Factor investing
  • Fama & French (1992) — Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns.
  • Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) — Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers.
  • Fama & French (1993) — Common Risk Factors in Stocks and Bonds.
  • Carhart (1997) — Persistence in Mutual Fund Performance.
  • Fama & French (2015) — Five-Factor Asset Pricing Model.
Limits of trading and active management
  • Carhart (1997) — Persistence in Mutual Fund Performance.
  • Barber & Odean (2000) — Trading Is Hazardous to Your Wealth.
  • Barber & Odean (2001) — Overconfidence and Excessive Trading.
  • SPIVA Reports (ongoing) — Active funds vs benchmark performance.
Data collection
  • Cowles Commission (1930s) — Early systematic market datasets.
  • CRSP (1960–) — Long-run U.S. stock return database (data back to 1926).
  • Shiller (1981) — Do Stock Prices Move Too Much to Be Justified by Subsequent Changes in Dividends?
  • Dimson, Marsh & Staunton (2023) — Global Investment Returns Yearbook.
